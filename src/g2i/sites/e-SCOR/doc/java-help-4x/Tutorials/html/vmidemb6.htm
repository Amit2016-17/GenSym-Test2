Analyzing Opportunity Costs of Different Service Levels

You now have all the information you need to determine the opportunity cost of providing different service levels-on-time performance-to different customers, based on different levels of consumer demand. You can use this information to help make key business decisions, such as:

The relative price you charge different customers, based on variation in consumer demand.
The contract terms you negotiate with difference customers, based on target service levels.
Whether to carry less inventory to cover a smaller variation in demand for certain customers and contract with third-party vendors to cover occasional inventory shortages.

To analyze the opportunity costs of providing different levels of service, you run multiple simulations to obtain a series of data points that compare inventory holding costs with performance levels for each consumer.

For example, you might run five simulations that use replenishment levels that yield 100% on-time performance, five simulations that use replenishment levels that yield approximately a 90% on-time performance, five simulations that use replenishment levels that yield approximately 80% on-time performance, and so on. You could then plot these points on a graph to produce a curve that determines inventory holding cost for different levels of on-time performance. Each customer will have a different curve, because each customer has different variation in its demand.

You can run multiple simulations by using the Scenario Manager, which lets you configure a script that sets parameter values for each simulation. For details, see "Using the Scenario Manager with the Central Distributor Tutorial Model".

To compute opportunity cost, you:

Convert Inventory Days of Supply into a dollar amount by multiplying by the Net Selling Price to obtain the Inventory Cost.
Convert the Inventory Cost into opportunity cost by multiplying by the potential return on investment, for example, 12%.

4 Now, answer the following questions to help you understand the implications of these results and to help you answer key business decisions around pricing and service contracts:
Explain the variation in opportunity cost at the lowest level of service.
Hint: Customer A and Customer C have approximately the same opportunity cost at the lowest level of service because they are both ordering the same amount of products, on average (100 units), whereas Customer B is ordering twice as much, on average (200 units). The variation is due to variation in demand.
Why does it cost Customer A and Customer B so much more to obtain 100% on-time performance than it costs Customer C?
Hint: It costs Customers A and B so much more because of the variation in demand. Because Customer C has no variation, it can provide 100% on-time performance at significantly lower cost than the other two customers.
What is range of opportunity costs for each of the three customers? How do they compare?
Hint: The ranges are $57m, $60m, and $22.3m for Customers A, B, and C, respectively. The ranges for Customers A and B are approximately the same and approximately twice as high as the range for Customer C, again due to variation in demand.
What is the incremental cost of going from a 98% service level to a 100% service level for each of the three customers? What does this tell you about relative pricing for each of the three customers to provide a 100% service level?
Hint: It costs $37.15m, $23.75m, and $19.6m for Customers A, B, and C, respectively, to go from a 98% to a 100% service level. By comparing the relative costs of providing 100% service levels for each customer, you can determine the relative price to charge each customer. In particular, you can charge Customer A approximately twice as much as you charge Customer C (37.15/19.6), and you can charge Customer B approximately 20% more than you charge Customer C (23.75/19.6).
Based on your answer to the question above, with which customer might it make most sense to negotiate a lower service level? Why?
Hint: Because the incremental cost of going from a 98% to a 100% service level is so much higher for Customer A, you might consider negotiating a lower price for lower levels of service. To make up for the difference, you might negotiate a contract with a third-party vendor to cover inventory shortfall for significantly less than the incremental $37.15m it would cost to provide the highest level of service yourself.
